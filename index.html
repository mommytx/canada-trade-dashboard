AI Insight 01 Concentration Risk

Three provinces carry nearly all of Canada's U.S. exposure

Ontario, Alberta, and Quebec together account for 82.9% of Canada's total exports to the United States ($480.1B of $579.0B). The map below shows ten provinces colored by dependence, but the raw dollars are extraordinarily concentrated. Canada's economic response to U.S. policy is effectively determined by the manufacturing base of Ontario, Alberta's oil & gas sector, and Quebec's aerospace/aluminum complex. The other seven provinces combined ($98.9B) export less to the U.S. than Alberta alone.

AI Insight 02 Dollar-Loss Paradox

The biggest percentage losers aren't the biggest dollar losers

Autos cratered -42.8% YoY — the kind of number that makes headlines. But because Oil & Gas has a base four times larger, its comparatively modest -14.4% drop erased more absolute dollars from the Canadian economy than autos did. If you're allocating policy attention by dollar impact rather than shock headlines, energy — not autos — is the top priority.

AI Insight 03 Winners vs Losers

The "diversification story" is mathematically negligible

Seven sectors grew during the tariff period. Green cells in the treemap below suggest resilience. But the scale tells a different story: all seven growth industries combined added only +$513M, while the 18 declining industries lost -$15.7B. The "winners" offset 3.2% of the losses. When commentators point to rising recyclable metal wholesalers (+55%) or vegetable oils (+11%) as signs of Canadian adaptation, the numbers show adaptation is happening but at a scale 30× too small to matter.

AI Insight 04 Recovery Trajectory

10 months post-shock, exports are still below the pre-tariff baseline

The Main Trend tab shows a steady climb from the April 2025 low of $31.9B. But the climb has not reached the starting line. Feb 2026 came in at $35.2B — still 2.5% below the 2024 baseline average of $36.1B. Recovery has been real but incomplete: roughly 78% of the initial shock has been recovered, and the remaining 22% appears to be structural rather than transient. This is the shape of a permanent demand shift, not a temporary disruption.

AI Insight 05 CUSMA Countdown

The clock is running on the single most consequential trade event of 2026

Every tariff discussed in this dashboard exists within a specific legal framework. The Canada-U.S.-Mexico Agreement is currently under its six-year mandatory review, with a decision deadline of July 1, 2026. The outcome determines whether CUSMA continues (preserving the ~89% of Canadian exports currently tariff-exempt under compliant trade) or enters a 10-year wind-down.
—
Days until July 1, 2026
What's at stake: CUSMA-compliant goods currently avoid the 25% steel/aluminum and auto tariffs. If review fails, the IEEPA tariffs struck down by the U.S. Supreme Court (Feb 2026) could return under different legal authority, removing the compliance safety valve that protects ~89% of current cross-border trade.

U.S. trade vs Mexico trade
CUSMA is a three-party agreement, but for Canada it is effectively a bilateral one. Mexico is Canada's 5th-largest export market — smaller than China, Japan, and the U.K. Canada-Mexico exports contracted 3.5% in 2024 (Global Affairs Canada). If CUSMA collapses, Mexico cannot absorb a meaningful fraction of displaced U.S.-bound exports.
Why this dashboard matters now: Every number shown assumes CUSMA holds. Negotiators have fewer than 11 weeks to reach terms — and there is no viable trilateral substitute if they fail.
